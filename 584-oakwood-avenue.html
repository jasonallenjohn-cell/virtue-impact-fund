The Thesis

Federal capital builds it. The fund owns the land.

Entitled Toronto land, a 40% Build Canada Homes grant and a CMHC MLI Select mortgage are modelled to combine so a seniors retirement home finances on its own income — with the fund contributing land, not cash. The same land-unlock structure the manager pursues at other pipeline sites, including 8440 and Keele Street.

Step 01

Contribute land, not cash

The fund puts in the $5M Oakwood land. No construction equity, no build risk — the land is the position.

Step 02

Federal capital fills the gap

A 40% Build Canada Homes grant (no repayment) plus a CMHC MLI Select mortgage are modelled to fund the build, subject to grant approval, financing and appraisal.

Step 03

Contribute, hold, and own

The fund retains a minority interest, an income interest and a Year-20 buyout option. Terms are set out in the Offering Memorandum.

The Land-Unlock · The Fund's Position

Land in. A structure modelled around an early release of land value.

Amounts depend on grant approval, financing and appraisal and are not assured — the fund's interest remains at risk. Terms and modelled scenarios are described in the Offering Memorandum.

Why Oakwood

On the LRT, in a cultural district — and short on seniors housing.

Oakwood Avenue is a Major Street in Toronto's Little Jamaica Cultural District, directly on the new Eglinton Crosstown LRT. Seniors demand is deep and non-cyclical — while the affordable component and the at-grade Heal House answer a real local need and the politics of approval.

On the LRT

Steps from the Eglinton Crosstown (Oakwood station) — transit-rich, walkable, central.

Deep demand

An ageing city and almost no new affordable seniors housing — non-cyclical, resilient demand.

Approvable

The City wants height and at-grade community use — the Heal House delivers exactly that.
